Rivers & River Corridors

Rivers are life. The rivers of Saptari are like the veins of the human body, numerous and branching, carrying life to every corner of the district. From the mighty Sapta Koshi on the east to the Balan on the west, with the Khando, Mahuli, Jita, Ghordah and many seasonal streams between them, water has always written Saptari's story: its fertility, its floods, and its future.

The corridor idea

River corridors, continuous belts of embankment, greenery, walkways, ghats and managed wetlands along each river, can turn Saptari's flood liabilities into public assets: recreation, fisheries, groundwater recharge, tourism and land value. The campaign documents each river's corridor potential and takes stakeholders to the water's edge to see it for themselves. The convener's own river journeys, walking the Khado, the Koshi and the Chure hills with wildlife experts, geologists and fellow travellers, are archived under Resources below.

The Lakes of Saptari

Beyond its rivers, Saptari holds several lakes and wetlands. The convener has been quietly championing them for years, taking stakeholders, officials, national experts and well-wishers to the water's edge so the district's lake heritage earns the attention of the concerned authorities. A proposed Khado Lake and eco-tourism destination study is in the campaign archive (see the Library).

Kajali Baba Tal

Visited repeatedly with national experts and stakeholders to build the case for its conservation and development. Photos are archived under Resources.

Thakur Baba Tal

Explored and promoted over years, bringing stakeholders to coordinate with the local Ashram. Direct support to the Ashram's office and library included a full set of Vedic books funded in 2014 for NPR 25,000, along with office materials and an internet setup, and over NPR 2.5 lakh donated directly in 2015.

Gopit Ganga Lake, Bhedi Math

Another of the district's lakes, documented alongside its Math (monastery) as part of the same water-and-heritage record.

Setubandh's site visits — before development began

Team Setubandh's planning-commission members and experts walked these sites years before any formal proposal, including the Dighwa site later suggested by a government panel for a medical college. See Resources for the field record.

Koshi Tappu, tourism & flood response

Nepal's first Ramsar site is a recurring destination on the convener's journeys, alongside the reserve's warden and Tourism Board directors; he has also hosted the Nepal Tourism Board and the Rafting Association in Saptari, with Saptari Next providing local facilitation, food, transport and hosting. The same network responds when the rivers turn, as in the flood relief work carried out in Belhi Tilathi and elsewhere in the district.

Ghordah's own story, the Sangam Ghat at Majhau and its lake and hydro potential, lives on the Boria village module.

Mithila Heritage & Culture

Water shapes culture too. Through the Maithili Wikimedians group and on his own travels, the convener has advocated for Saptari's language, culture, food and land resources in colleges across the region, and documented the deeper Mithila heritage the district shares with Darbhanga and Patna across the border.

Mithila akshar T-shirt — language advocacy

Campaign merchandise carrying the Mithila script (akshar) itself, wearable language advocacy produced with Tshirt Nepal.

Nari Bikash Kendra, Janakpur

Visited on every trip to Janakpur, always buying something as a small gesture of support toward the women's craft centre.

Reviving the Maths of Mithila

The Maths, or monasteries, of Mithila are, in the convener's view, a binding moral force for the region's prosperity; their revival is documented across Darbhanga and Patna in India, at Ghordah, and at a Math in Siraha.

Maulapur, Simraungarh

Tracing the golden era of Mithila at Simraungarh, once the seat of the Karnat and Oiniwar dynasties.

WikiAwareness & Wiki Loves Earth

Open-knowledge outreach at the campaign's own drawing room, ChaiCharcha, and beyond, including Wiki Loves Earth in Kathmandu.

Local agriculture, food & cuisine

Promoting and innovating in local produce and Mithila cuisine, including the story of Achar Freak, a homegrown pickle brand carrying the taste of Mithila to a wider audience (see the About page for its JCI connection).
